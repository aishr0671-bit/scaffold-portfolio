01   About the Team

About Us

Who we are

We are three second-year undergraduate students studying Biomedical Engineering at SRM Institute of Science and Technology, Chennai. Biomedical Engineering is the field that combines biology and medicine with engineering — designing devices, materials, and systems that help the human body heal.

This project is our academic mini project, where we were asked to design a biodegradable implant. Instead of choosing a common topic, we chose to tackle one of the hardest unsolved problems in medicine: why the brain cannot repair itself after a stroke — and what we could do about it.

Why this problem?

Every year, millions of people survive strokes but are left permanently disabled — unable to move their arm, walk normally, or speak. Rehabilitation like physiotherapy helps, but only so much. The reason recovery has a limit is not widely known: after a stroke, the brain builds a chemical wall around the damaged area that physically blocks any new nerve connections from forming. No existing treatment removes this wall.

We designed a material that goes in, breaks down that wall, guides the brain's own nerve fibres to reconnect — and then dissolves itself. Nothing is left behind. No battery, no device to remove, no second surgery.

About this project

This is a research proposal — a detailed, scientifically justified plan for a new treatment. We have not built it in a lab yet; we have designed every component, justified every material choice, and planned every experiment needed to prove it works.

Project Overview

A Glial Scar-Disrupting, MMP-Responsive Biodegradable Cortical Scaffold for Peri-Infarct Neuroplasticity Restoration

An injectable hyaluronic acid hydrogel crosslinked with MMP-cleavable PVGLIG peptides, loaded with trehalose-stabilized chABC microspheres and laminin-tethered BDNF. The scaffold dismantles the post-stroke glial scar using the brain's own enzyme activity as the release trigger — then degrades completely, leaving only restored cortical tissue.

We designed a gel that is injected into the brain, finds the damaged zone on its own, releases a medicine that dissolves the chemical barrier blocking recovery, guides new nerve fibres to grow — and then completely disappears within four months, leaving only healed tissue.

04   Methodology

Preparation & Procedure

Overview
Five things happen, in order, automatically

No one presses a button. No external device controls any of this. The damaged brain tissue itself signals the material to act — and when the damage is healed, the signals stop and the material dissolves.

The scaffold is made of hyaluronic acid — a molecule the brain already produces naturally, so the body does not recognise it as foreign. This gel carries two active ingredients locked inside tiny spheres: an enzyme called chondroitinase ABC, which dissolves the chemical barrier blocking nerve repair; and a protein called BDNF, which tells new nerve fibres where to grow. The gel is injected as a liquid through a very fine needle and solidifies inside the brain in 90 seconds, conforming exactly to the damaged cavity. It is so soft (stiffness 0.3–0.8 kPa) that the brain cannot even detect it is there.

01
Day 0 · Delivery
The gel is injected and solidifies in place
A neurosurgeon injects the liquid scaffold through a fine needle into the space left by the stroke. Within 90 seconds, body heat turns it into a soft gel that perfectly fills the cavity. It is so gentle it does not disturb the surrounding brain tissue at all — no stitches, no hard implant, no damage from insertion.
02
Weeks 1–4 · Detection
The gel finds the chemical barrier automatically
The damaged brain releases large quantities of enzymes called MMPs — the brain's own attempt to repair itself, which fails because the barrier is too dense. Our gel is designed to respond specifically to these enzymes: wherever MMP levels are highest (exactly at the barrier), the gel begins to open up and release its medicine. It targets itself — no GPS, no electronics, no programmer required.
03
Weeks 2–6 · Clearing
The chemical barrier is dissolved
The released enzyme (chondroitinase ABC) dismantles the proteins that make up the barrier — one by one, like cutting the threads of a net. As the barrier dissolves, the nerve fibres that were previously stopped at its edge begin growing forward again into the cleared space. This is the moment where recovery, which was biologically impossible before, becomes possible.
04
Weeks 6–10 · Reconnection
Nerve fibres are guided to their destination
Simply clearing the barrier is not enough — growing nerve fibres also need direction, or they will wander randomly. The gel releases a guidance protein called BDNF that creates a chemical trail pointing toward the healthy brain tissue. Nerve fibres follow this trail like a path through a forest, reaching the undamaged neurons and forming new connections. The brain's circuits begin to rebuild.
05
Weeks 10–16 · Disappearance
The scaffold dissolves completely
Once the nerve fibres have reconnected, the gel is no longer needed. The body's natural enzymes break it down into harmless water-soluble fragments that are safely absorbed. By week 16, there is no trace of the scaffold left — no foreign material, no device to surgically remove, no long-term immune reaction. Only the recovered brain tissue and its new connections remain.

06   Result

Millions of stroke survivors reach a ceiling in their recovery that no current treatment can break through. That ceiling is the chemical wall the brain builds around its own damage. Our scaffold is designed to dissolve that wall — automatically, precisely, and temporarily — using only the brain's own chemistry as the signal. It delivers its medicine exactly where the damage is, guides nerve fibres to reconnect across the cleared space, and then vanishes completely, leaving no device, no scar, and no second surgery behind. We believe this represents what the next generation of stroke treatment could look like.

No battery  ·  No device to remove  ·  No drug flooding the entire body  ·  The damage itself tells the medicine where to go
